># Bathroom Renovation Safety: Dealing with Asbestos in Older Homes

**By DL Demolition Team** | Published: December 3, 2024 | 4 min read

---

Renovating a bathroom can be an exciting project, transforming an outdated space into a modern sanctuary. However, for owners of homes built before 1990, this excitement can be tempered by a hidden danger: asbestos. Bathrooms in older homes are one of the most common places to find asbestos-containing materials (ACMs), and disturbing these materials without the proper precautions can have serious health consequences.

This article will highlight the essential safety considerations when renovating bathrooms in pre-1990s homes, focusing on asbestos identification and the correct protocols for its management and removal.

## Why Was Asbestos Used in Bathrooms?

Asbestos was a popular choice for wet areas like bathrooms for several key reasons:

*   **Water Resistance:** Asbestos fibres are hydrophobic, meaning they repel water, making them ideal for use in materials designed for wet environments.
*   **Durability:** ACMs are strong and long-lasting.
*   **Fire Resistance and Insulation:** Asbestos provided excellent thermal and acoustic insulation.

## Common Places to Find Asbestos in a Bathroom

If your home was built before 1990, you could find asbestos in a number of places in your bathroom:

*   **Cement Sheet Walls and Floors:** Often used as a backing for tiles or as the primary wall and floor sheeting.
*   **Vinyl Floor Tiles:** Both the tiles themselves and the adhesive used to stick them down can contain asbestos.
*   **Pipe Lagging:** Insulation around hot water pipes.
*   **Textured Ceilings:** Popcorn ceilings or other textured finishes were often made with asbestos.
*   **Sealants and Joint Compounds:** The compounds used around baths, showers, and sinks could contain asbestos.

## The Dangers of Disturbing Asbestos in a Renovation

The primary risk with asbestos comes from the inhalation of its microscopic fibres. When ACMs are left undisturbed and are in good condition, they generally do not pose a significant health risk. However, the very nature of a renovation—demolishing walls, pulling up floors, and cutting into materials—can easily release these dangerous fibres into the air.

Once airborne, these fibres can be inhaled and become lodged in the lungs, leading to serious diseases such as asbestosis, lung cancer, and mesothelioma, which can take decades to develop.

## Safety Protocols for Renovating an Older Bathroom

If you are planning to renovate a bathroom in a pre-1990s home, following these safety protocols is not just recommended—it is essential.

### Step 1: Assume Asbestos is Present

Until you have professional confirmation to the contrary, you must assume that any suspicious materials contain asbestos. Do not proceed with any demolition or renovation work.

### Step 2: Professional Identification and Testing

Engage a licensed asbestos assessor to inspect your bathroom. They will be able to identify potential ACMs and can take small samples for testing at a NATA-accredited laboratory. This is the only way to be 100% certain.

### Step 3: Do Not DIY Removal

Even if you are an experienced DIYer, asbestos removal is a job for licensed professionals. The risks are too high, and the legal requirements are strict. In Queensland, a licence is required to remove more than 10 square metres of non-friable asbestos or any amount of friable asbestos.

### Step 4: Engage a Licensed Asbestos Removalist

If asbestos is confirmed, a licensed asbestos removalist will need to be engaged. They will:

*   Develop a detailed asbestos removal control plan.
*   Seal off the work area to prevent contamination of the rest of your home.
*   Use specialised equipment and techniques to remove the ACMs safely.
*   Decontaminate the area thoroughly.
*   Arrange for the legal disposal of the asbestos waste.

## What If Asbestos is Found and is in Good Condition?

In some cases, if the ACM is in good, stable condition and will not be disturbed by the renovation, it may be possible to leave it in place and work around it. For example, you might be able to tile over existing asbestos cement sheeting. However, this decision should only be made after a thorough risk assessment by a qualified professional. You must also consider the long-term implications, as the asbestos will still be present and will need to be managed during any future renovations.

## Conclusion

A bathroom renovation should be a cause for excitement, not a health risk. By being aware of the potential for asbestos in older homes and taking the appropriate safety precautions, you can ensure your project is completed safely and successfully. Always prioritise the health of your family and your renovation team by engaging licensed professionals for the identification and removal of any suspected asbestos. It is a decision that provides not just a beautiful new bathroom, but also invaluable peace of mind.
